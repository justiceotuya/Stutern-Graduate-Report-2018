About Stutern Graduate Report

For the second year in a row, Stutern has tapped into the data of recent Nigerian graduates (those who graduated within the last five years) to reveal insights into Nigerian graduates employment status, as well as the hiring and the education market more generally. Because Stutern facilitates the transition from the four walls of the schools to the labor market for thousands of young Nigerians, we have a unique visibility across a variety of positions and companies.
Since our release of the debut edition of Stutern’s Nigerian Graduate Report, youth unemployment in Nigeria has reached its peak. This second edition of the Nigerian Graduate Report is part of our larger effort to bring more transparency into the state of graduate employment in Nigeria. Ultimately, we want to use this report to prompt all stakeholders to be aware of the importance of skills development and job creation to build a more productive youth population.

EMPLOYMENT

EMPLOYMENT STATUS

The majority of recent graduates aspire to find a job as soon as they graduate, but do they actually find one? According to our survey, majority of graduates who completed their studies within the last five years are currently unemployed (28.9% ) while 26.3% are working in a full time paid employment. About 1 in 6 respondents (16%) reported that they are observing the one year mandatory National Youth Service Corps (NYSC) program. While 13.4% are practicing entrepreneurship, freelance and other self employment activities, 8% are involved in voluntary and other unpaid employment. The remaining 7.2% are engaged or preparing for further studies.

EMPLOYMENT STATUS BY GENDER

Recent female graduates in Nigeria are more likely to report they are unemployed (with 32.3% of female respondents stating they are unemployed ) than their male counterparts (25.65%). The other activity where female recent graduates have a leading ratio is in observing their National Youth Service Corps program. Women slip behind men when it comes to reporting having found a full time paid employment, voluntary or other unpaid work or an internship, self employment/freelance/entrepreneurship.

SALARIES

STARTING & CURRENT SALARY BY GENDER

Our analysis shows that the ratio of female graduate earners reduces as salary raises. This is true in both the first job and current job salary for recent graduates. Also, the only period when the ratio of female graduate earners is above the proportion of male earners is when earnings are under N20,000 ($55.6).

SALARY DISTRIBUTION

3 out of 5 (about 60%) of Nigerian graduates earn less than 50,000 ($139) as their first job monthly salary. Upon getting a later job, that number falls to 2 out of 5 (a little above 40%). Overall, most recent graduates earn between N20,000 to N49,999 ($56 to $139) in their first job after graduation while in for their later job salary, most earn between N50,000 to N99,999.

WELFARE

EMPLOYMENT STATUS

2 in 10 full-time employed graduates within the last five years have been about to either rent an apartment or buy a car from the salary gotten from their job (first job and current job combined).

MEDIUM OF COMMUTING TO WORK

Most of the recent graduates from our survey travels to work, through the use of commercial buses (59.6%). The use of cars and tricycles (Keke Napep/Maruwa) comes second and third as the most utilized channels of getting to work (10.9% and 8%). On the other end, the three least employed medium of journeying to work are raid hailing services (like Uber and Taxify), bicycles and boat at 2.6%, 0.32% and 0.06% respectively.

EDUCATION

MOST EMPLOYED GRADUATES COURSES

Computer Science, Accountancy, and Economics were the top 3 courses studied by respondents who indicated to be working in a paid full-time employment. Also, it’s important to note that, from our survey, about 50% of recent graduates who are in full-time paid employment studied from the top 10 courses listed.

EDUCATION SATISFACTION

Overall, 69.4% of the recent graduates we surveyed believed their academic experience prepared them for employment. On the other hand only 28.6% of employers believe most of their employees were well prepared for employment by their different courses of study. Also, most graduates believed their academic experience prepared them for further study than for employment at 86.82%.

EMPLOYABILITY SKILLS

More than 50% of the graduates responded that their education did not prepare them with skills such as knowledge of the skill(s) they studied, spoken communication skills, written communication skills and the ability to solve complex problems.

OTHERS

HOW GRADUATES GOT THE JOBS

From our survey, graduates were 10 times more likely to find a job through their personal contacts than through their academic institutions, 5 times more likely to find a job than through traditional media and 2 times more likely than through either social media network or online job sites.

EMPLOYING INDUSTRIES

Our research shows that, although the percentage of graduates that work in the education sector plummets when graduates change from their first jobs to their ensuing jobs. Nevertheless, the education industry still employs majority of recent graduates during their first or subsequent jobs. The industries that experienced the most increase in the number of recent graduates upon a switch from first jobs are media, technology and healthcare sectors. Education, telecommunications and the construction industry. The industries that were most stable (no rapid fall or rise) includes agriculture, transportation and consulting industry.
